How to Write an Amazing Cover Letter
By Brett Ellis, PMP | November 6, 2018 (Updated March 9, 2021)

The cover letter gives you the opportunity to provide information that may not be explicitly addressed in your resume. Benefits of a good cover letter include:

Separating you from the competition.

Providing context for your fit with the company.

Addressing possible questions employers might have about your resume.

Showcasing your passion and personality.

The Strategy: The AIDA Model
Adopting a marketing mindset is key. The AIDA model (Attention, Interest, Desire, and Action) provides a structure where each letter represents a paragraph:

1. ATTENTION (Paragraph 1)
Your goal is to grab the reader's attention immediately. Avoid standard openings like "I am applying for...". Instead, try:

Telling a Story: Share an impressive accomplishment or show passion for the position. For example, a realtor might describe closing a $3 million deal after a late-night call.

Addressing Current News: Show you’ve done your research by mentioning industry trends or company news.

Getting Creative: Use a style that motivates the reader to keep reading.

2. INTEREST (Paragraph 2)
Maintain interest by focusing on the employer’s needs rather than your own. Use "you," "your," and "yours" more frequently to increase readability.

Less Effective: "I am looking for..."

More Effective: "Your company can benefit by..."

3. DESIRE (Paragraph 3)
Create a desire for your employment by connecting your past accomplishments to the major points in the job description. Provide specific examples, such as how a self-starter attitude saved a previous company time and money.

Pro Tip: Use hyperlinks to connect your past work samples (like newsletters or data reports) directly to their needs.

4. ACTION (Paragraph 4)
Compel the employer to reach out. Wrap up by providing contact options without being too demanding. For example: "I would love the opportunity to discuss your needs in person or over a phone call".

Addressing "Red Flags"
Don't hide potential concerns like career changes, lack of direct experience, or resume gaps. Address them upfront with confidence by highlighting your transferable skills and explaining how your experiences have prepared you for the role.
